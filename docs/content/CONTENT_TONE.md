# Content Tone

## georgeungureanu.doctor

---

## The Voice of This Website

The voice is the doctor speaking directly to the patient.

Not the hospital. Not the institution. Not the press office. The doctor.

A doctor who is excellent at their craft, genuinely concerned about the patient in front of them, and has the rare gift of explaining complex things in plain language without condescension.

---

## Core Tone Attributes

### Calm

The language is unhurried. Sentences are not rushed. Ideas are given room. The voice does not panic, does not create urgency, does not use language that accelerates anxiety.

Even when describing serious conditions, the tone is steady. "This is serious, and here is how we approach it" — not "This is alarming and requires immediate action."

### Clear

Every sentence earns its place. No padding. No filler. No sentences that repeat what the previous sentence said in different words.

Medical terms are either replaced with plain language or immediately explained:
- Not: "The procedure involves a craniotomy."
- Instead: "The procedure involves temporarily removing a portion of the skull to access the brain — a technique called a craniotomy."

### Warm

The language acknowledges that the reader is a person in a difficult situation, not a clinical case to be processed.

This warmth is not forced. It is not "Hey, you've got this!" positivity. It is the natural warmth of a doctor who takes the time to explain, who asks if the patient understood, who treats the person as an individual.

### Direct

The voice does not hedge unnecessarily or hide behind institutional language.

- Not: "In certain cases, depending on individual patient circumstances, it may sometimes be appropriate to consider..."
- Instead: "Most patients are able to return home within three to five days."

Directness builds trust. Hedging erodes it.

### Precise

When precision matters — in describing procedures, recovery timelines, risks — the language is accurate. We do not oversimplify to the point of misleading.

The balance: precise enough to be true, plain enough to be understood.

---

## What the Voice Is Not

### Not Promotional

The voice does not sell. It does not use phrases like:
- "World-class care"
- "State-of-the-art facilities"
- "Dedicated to excellence"
- "Best-in-class outcomes"

These phrases are meaningless to a patient in distress. They signal marketing, not medicine.

### Not Intimidating

The voice does not demonstrate expertise through complexity. It demonstrates expertise through clarity.

A doctor who explains a complex condition simply is more impressive than one who explains it complexly.

### Not Paternalistic

The voice respects the patient's intelligence. It explains things that need explaining, but it does not talk down to the reader.

- Not: "As you may not be aware, the brain is a complex organ..."
- Instead: "The brain controls everything from movement to memory — which is why surgery in this area requires exceptional precision."

### Not Falsely Cheerful

The voice does not minimize difficulty. When something is hard, it says so — and then explains how it is managed.

- Not: "Recovery is easy and most patients feel great quickly!"
- Instead: "Recovery from this procedure typically takes four to six weeks. The first two weeks are the most demanding. Most patients are back to normal activities by week six."

### Not Passive

The voice uses active constructions. The doctor acts; the patient is not merely acted upon.

- Not: "Surgery will be performed."
- Instead: "Dr. Ungureanu performs this procedure using minimally invasive techniques."

## Sentence-Level Rules

### Sentence Length

Mix short and medium sentences. Avoid long compound sentences in patient-facing body text.

- Maximum reading comfort: average sentence length of 15–18 words
- Use short sentences (under 10 words) to land important points
- Avoid run-on sentences connected by multiple "and" or "which" clauses

### Paragraph Length

Patient-facing paragraphs: maximum 4–5 lines on desktop.

Long paragraphs signal that the content hasn't been edited. They also exhaust anxious readers.

### Reading Level

Target: Grade 8–10 reading level for condition and treatment content.

Not because patients are unsophisticated — because patients under stress read at lower effective reading levels, and clarity is never an insult.

## Medical Language Rules

### Rule 1: Define Before Use

If a medical term must be used, define it immediately:

> "You may experience dysesthesia — abnormal sensations like tingling, burning, or numbness — in the affected area during recovery."

### Rule 2: Prefer Plain Equivalents When Accurate

| Medical | Plain |
|---------|-------|
| Craniotomy | Opening in the skull |
| Resection | Surgical removal |
| Prognosis | Expected outcome |
| Contraindicated | Not recommended / not safe |
| Idiopathic | Without a known cause |
| Benign | Non-cancerous |
| Malignant | Cancerous |

### Rule 3: Abbreviations

No unexplained abbreviations. Ever. If an abbreviation must be used (e.g., MRI), write it out in full on first use in every document:

> "You will need a magnetic resonance imaging scan (MRI) before the consultation."

### Rule 4: Statistics and Numbers

When providing statistics or outcome data:
- Present them in a way that is reassuring where appropriate, or honest where necessary
- Provide context: "9 out of 10 patients" is more meaningful than "90% of cases"
- Never present statistics without context that helps the patient interpret them

---

## Content Checklist

Before any content is finalized, check:

- [ ] Does every medical term have a plain-language explanation?
- [ ] Does every section tell the patient what to do next?
- [ ] Is the reading level appropriate (Grade 8–10)?
- [ ] Are all abbreviations expanded on first use?
- [ ] Is the tone calm and direct, not alarming or hedging?
- [ ] Are there any marketing phrases that should be replaced with honest language?
- [ ] Does the content acknowledge the patient's emotional reality?
- [ ] Is every sentence earning its place?

## Language Examples

### Describing a Condition

**Wrong:**
> "Meningioma is a tumor arising from the meninges, the membranous layers surrounding the brain and spinal cord. While typically benign, the mass effect of the tumor can produce significant neurological symptomatology depending on its location and rate of growth."

**Right:**
> "A meningioma is a growth that develops on the protective covering of the brain. Most meningiomas are non-cancerous and grow slowly. Depending on their size and location, they can press on parts of the brain and cause symptoms like headaches, changes in vision, or weakness. The goal of treatment is to relieve that pressure."

### Describing a Procedure

**Wrong:**
> "Dr. Ungureanu is renowned for his exceptional mastery of minimally invasive neurosurgical techniques, consistently achieving superior patient outcomes."

**Right:**
> "Dr. Ungureanu uses minimally invasive techniques whenever they are appropriate for the patient's condition. Smaller incisions typically mean less pain, shorter hospital stays, and a faster return to daily life."

### Describing Recovery

**Wrong:**
> "Recovery is a journey that varies from patient to patient. It is important to follow your physician's guidance carefully."

**Right:**
> "Most patients go home within two to four days after this procedure. Full recovery typically takes six to eight weeks. You will have a follow-up appointment two weeks after surgery to check your progress and discuss when you can safely return to work."
