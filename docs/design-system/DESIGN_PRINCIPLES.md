## The Governing Principle

Every design decision answers one question first:

> "Does this help a patient feel informed, reassured, understood, and guided?"

Design is not decoration. Design is communication in service of the patient.

---

## The Seven Design Principles

### 1. Clarity Over Complexity

If two design approaches exist — one simpler, one more sophisticated — choose the simpler one unless sophistication meaningfully serves the patient.

- Use clear typographic hierarchy that guides the eye without effort
- Use section headings that answer questions, not describe content categories
- Use visual cues (icons, color, spacing) to distinguish content types without explanation
- Remove anything that does not help the patient understand or act

**Test:** Can a patient who has never visited a medical website navigate this page without confusion?

---

### 2. Calm Over Clever

This website is visited by people in distress. The design must lower anxiety, not compete for attention.

- No animations that demand attention
- No color contrast that creates visual tension
- No layout complexity that creates cognitive load
- No popups, modals, or interruptive elements (except accessibility-required focus traps)
- Motion, when used, is subtle, slow, and purposeful

**Test:** Does viewing this page lower or raise a visitor's stress level?

---

### 3. Space as a Signal

Whitespace is not empty space. Whitespace is the visual equivalent of a calm, unhurried doctor who gives you time to speak.

- Generous line-height in body text (1.7 minimum — non-negotiable)
- Generous section padding (never cramped — 80px minimum on desktop)
- Breathing room between typographic elements
- Content that is not fighting for space against other content
- The warm cream backgrounds amplify the feeling of space — they do not create it alone

**Test:** Does this layout feel like there is room to think, or does it feel rushed?

---

### 4. Type as Architecture

Typography carries 80% of the communication work on this website. Treat it accordingly.

- Establish a clear visual hierarchy: headline (Lora) → subhead → body (Inter) → caption → label
- Use font size, weight, and color (not size alone) to communicate hierarchy
- Body text is never smaller than 17px on desktop, 16px on mobile
- Line length is never wider than 68 characters for reading comfort — this is a hard constraint
- All type choices are made in Elementor Global Typography — never overridden locally
- Lora is the editorial voice (authority + warmth). Inter is the informational voice (clarity + precision). Neither role bleeds into the other.

**Test:** Can a patient quickly understand the structure of a page without reading it?

---

### 5. Patient Journey Over Page Layout

Every page has a patient at the start and an action at the end. Design the path between them, not the page as an object.

- Every page has a clear primary action (book an appointment, read more, contact)
- Navigation supports the patient's question flow, not the doctor's professional categories
- CTAs are calm and directive, not urgent or pressuring ("Book a consultation" not "Contact us now!")
- The appointment pathway is never more than one click from any content page

**Test:** If a patient arrives on this page and wants to take the next step, can they do so in one click?

---

### 6. Mobile as the Primary Experience

Most patients search on their phones. The mobile experience is not a scaled-down desktop — it is the primary design context.

- Design for 375px width first
- Touch targets are minimum 44×44px
- Font sizes are readable without zoom
- Spacing scales appropriately for touch interaction
- Navigation collapses cleanly without loss of function

**Test:** Is this page fully usable and readable on a phone with one hand?

---

### 7. Academic Restraint

Sophistication is achieved through restraint, not complexity. The design references world-class medical institutions — institutions that communicate authority through what they choose not to do.

- Two typefaces maximum
- Global color palette only — no local overrides
- No decorative elements without semantic purpose
- No visual noise: gradients, textures, patterns, and overlapping elements only when meaningful
- Grid consistency across all pages — same rhythm, same logic

**Test:** Could this design appear on the website of a leading medical institution without looking out of place?

---

## Design Decisions Framework

When making a design decision, apply this sequence:

1. **Does it serve the patient?** If no, discard.
2. **Does it reduce cognitive load or add to it?** If it adds, reconsider.
3. **Does it maintain system consistency?** If no, use the existing system.
4. **Does it belong in the global design system?** If yes, add it there. Never create one-offs.

## What "Premium" Means Here

Premium does not mean expensive-looking. Premium means:

- Nothing wasted
- Nothing missing
- Everything in the right place
- Immediately trustworthy
- No effort required from the visitor to understand
- The warmth is felt before the content is read

The most premium design in this context is the one that makes the most complex medical situation feel manageable by a frightened person at midnight.

---

## The Direction B+ Synthesis Principle

This design system is built on a three-part synthesis:

**Warmth from Direction B** — The color temperature, the cream backgrounds, the sage accent, the literary serif: these create a space that feels human before it says a word.

**Discipline from Direction A** — The strict grid, the precise typographic hierarchy, the restrained section rhythm, the information architecture: these ensure that warmth never becomes vagueness.

**Respect for content from Direction C** — The reading column constraints, the generous leading, the careful treatment of long-form educational content: these honor the patient who is reading carefully because their health depends on it.

When these three forces pull against each other, Direction B always resolves the tension: warmth is the primary register. Discipline and information respect are in service of warmth, not competing with it.

---

## Reference Standard

When uncertain about a design decision, ask two questions in sequence:

**First:** Would a patient who arrived at this page frightened leave feeling slightly less frightened — before reading a word?

**Second:** Would this appear without embarrassment alongside the editorial quality of the *New England Journal of Medicine* website, Mayo Clinic, or Johns Hopkins Medicine?

If the answer to the first question is yes and the second is no: simplify the design until both are yes.
If the answer to the first is no: the design is wrong regardless of its sophistication.

The standard is not visual achievement. The standard is the patient's emotional state after encountering this design.
